PART TWO / THEATRES OF OPERATION

Where the Air War
Was Fought

PAGE 37 — 58
REF. TH/02-T
MAPS UPDATED 2025
1940

Battle of Britain — Western Europe

The first decisive battle fought entirely in the air. RAF Fighter Command's Hurricanes and Spitfires denied the Luftwaffe air superiority over the Channel, ending Hitler's plan to invade Britain.

→
1941–45

Strategic Bombing — Germany

RAF Bomber Command by night, the USAAF Eighth Air Force by day. Lancasters, Halifaxes, B-17s and B-24s waged a four-year campaign that killed 600,000 Germans and 79,000 Allied aircrew.

→
1941–43

Eastern Front — USSR

Yakovlev and Lavochkin fighters, Ilyushin Shturmoviks, and the longest air war of the conflict. The Soviet Union eventually deployed more aircraft than any other belligerent.

→
1941–45

Pacific — Carrier War

Pearl Harbor, Coral Sea, Midway, the Philippine Sea. Naval aviation came of age aboard the decks of the IJN and US Navy. Wildcats, Hellcats, Avengers, Vals, Kates and the long-legged Zero.

→
1942–43

North Africa & Mediterranean

Desert Air Force operations from Egypt to Tunisia. Hurricanes, Kittyhawks and Bostons supporting the Eighth Army; a rehearsal for the close-air-support doctrine that would dominate Normandy.

→
1944–45

CBI — China, Burma, India

The forgotten theatre. AVG "Flying Tigers", USAAF B-29s flying "the Hump", and the longest sustained jungle air-supply operation in history.

→

PART FOUR / OPERATIONAL CHRONOLOGY

Six Years in
Twelve Engagements

PAGE 72 — 96
ENTRIES BY DATE OF
FIRST CONTACT
01 SEP 1939 — POLAND

Wieluń & the First Stuka Strike

At 04:40 hours Junkers Ju 87s of I./StG 76 attack the undefended Polish town of Wieluń, opening the air war that will not end for 2,194 days.

10 JUL — 31 OCT 1940 · UK

The Battle of Britain

RAF Fighter Command — 1,963 single-seat fighters and a brand-new radar network — defeats the Luftwaffe's attempt to win air superiority over southern England.

1,977 Luftwaffe aircraft lost 1,744 RAF aircraft lost ~3,000 aircrew killed
07 DEC 1941 — HAWAII

Pearl Harbor

353 IJN aircraft launched from six carriers strike Battleship Row. The action sinks four US battleships, kills 2,403, and brings the United States into the war.

04–07 JUN 1942 · MIDWAY

Midway — The Pacific Turning Point

SBD Dauntless dive-bombers from USS Enterprise and Yorktown sink four IJN fleet carriers in roughly six minutes. The IJN never recovers its strategic initiative.

4 IJN carriers sunk 248 IJN aircraft lost 1 US carrier (Yorktown) lost
17 AUG & 14 OCT 1943 — GERMANY

Schweinfurt — The Cost of Day Bombing

Two unescorted Eighth Air Force raids on the German ball-bearing industry lose 60 and then 77 B-17s in a single day apiece. Daylight precision bombing without long-range escort is suspended.

06 JUN 1944 — NORMANDY

Operation Overlord — Air Cover for D-Day

Some 11,500 Allied aircraft — Mustangs, Spitfires, Typhoons, P-47s, B-26s, gliders and tugs — fly more than 14,000 sorties over the Normandy beachhead. The Luftwaffe manages roughly 250.

JUL 1944 — GERMANY

Me 262 Enters Service

Erprobungskommando 262 receives its first jets. The Schwalbe — 870 km/h, four 30mm cannon — arrives too late and in too few numbers to alter the war's course.

13–15 FEB 1945 — DRESDEN

Dresden Firestorm

796 RAF Lancasters and 311 USAAF B-17s drop 3,900 tons of bombs in the most destructive single conventional raid of the European war.

06 + 09 AUG 1945 — JAPAN

Hiroshima & Nagasaki

B-29 Superfortresses Enola Gay and Bockscar drop atomic weapons on Hiroshima and Nagasaki. Imperial Japan surrenders six days later, ending the war in the Pacific.

12 MAJOR INSTITUTIONS
EUROPE — UK★★★★★

Imperial War Museum Duxford

Cambridgeshire, England · CB22 4QR

The largest collection of historic military aircraft in Europe, on the original RAF station from which Douglas Bader's wing flew during the Battle of Britain. Home to the famous "Sally B" — the only airworthy B-17 in Europe.

Holds: Spitfire (multiple), Hurricane, Lancaster, B-17, P-51, Bf 109 G, Avro Anson, T-6 Texan
NORTH AMERICA — USA★★★★★

National Museum of the USAF

Dayton, Ohio · Wright-Patterson AFB

The world's largest and oldest military aviation museum. Four enormous hangars house everything from a B-17F to the only surviving Bockscar — the B-29 that dropped the second atomic bomb.

Holds: Bockscar, Memphis Belle, P-51, P-38, P-47, B-24, B-25, Me 262, Fw 190
NORTH AMERICA — USA★★★★☆

Planes of Fame Air Museum

Chino, California · KCNO

Concentration of airworthy warbirds with one of the highest flight-hour records in the world. Hosts the only flying A6M Zero with its original Sakae engine.

Holds: A6M5 Zero (flying), P-51D, P-38, P-47, F4U Corsair, Yak-3
EUROPE — UK★★★★☆

RAF Museum London & Cosford

Hendon & Cosford · England

The official aircraft museum of the Royal Air Force. Hendon's Battle of Britain hall is the most complete collection of 1940 combatants in existence — including the only surviving Boulton-Paul Defiant.

Holds: Defiant, Spitfire I, Hurricane I, Lancaster, Halifax, Mosquito, Bf 109 E, Ju 87, He 111
OCEANIA — AUSTRALIA★★★★☆

Australian War Memorial — ANZAC Hall

Canberra, ACT

"G for George," the Lancaster that flew 90 sorties with No. 460 Sqn RAAF, hangs above visitors here. Home also to a Japanese Ki-43 Hayabusa and the famous Mephisto A7V tank.

Holds: Lancaster G-George, Spitfire VIII, Ki-43, Mosquito, Beaufort
EUROPE — GERMANY★★★★☆

Deutsches Technikmuseum

Berlin, Germany

The only original Ju 87 Stuka in Europe — recovered from the Aegean Sea — and a comprehensive Luftwaffe collection including a rare Ar 96 and Bü 181.

Holds: Ju 87, Junkers Ju 52, Bf 110, Arado Ar 96, He 162

PART EIGHT / FIELD GLOSSARY

The Language
of the Cockpit

APPENDIX A
24 ENTRIES
REVISED Q4 2025
Angels RAF
Altitude expressed in thousands of feet. "Angels Two-Zero" — twenty thousand feet.
Bandit USAAF
A confirmed hostile aircraft, as opposed to a "bogey" (unidentified).
Bingo Fuel
Minimum fuel needed to reach base. Past bingo and you are coming home cold.
Bounce
Surprise attack from above and astern. The vast majority of WWII fighter kills were bounces.
Box Formation USAAF
The defensive combat box adopted by B-17 groups — overlapping fields of .50-cal fire across an entire formation.
Buzz Number
The two- or three-digit identifier painted on USAAF aircraft for ground-control identification.
Chandelle
An ascending 180° climbing turn to reverse direction with maximum altitude gain.
Drop Tank
External jettisonable fuel tank. Without them the P-51 could not reach Berlin.
Erks RAF
Ground crew. From "AC" — aircraftman, the lowest RAF rank — pronounced "ack."
Experte LW
Luftwaffe term for an ace pilot. Required at least five confirmed kills.
Finger Four
The "Schwarm" formation — four aircraft in pairs, staggered like fingertips. Werner Mölders' lasting tactical contribution.
Gee & Oboe
British radio-navigation systems that let RAF Bomber Command find its targets at night.
Hedgehopping
Low-level flight at the height of the trees and hedgerows. Suicidal but effective.
Immelmann
Half-loop terminating in a half-roll. Reverses direction with altitude gain. Named for Max Immelmann.
Jericho-Trompete
The wind-driven sirens fitted to early Stukas to terrify those below.
Kanalkampf
"Channel Battle" — the opening phase of the Battle of Britain, July 1940.
Mae West
The inflatable life vest. Named for the actress for reasons obvious on inflation.
Pancake
Land. From the desired flat attitude on touchdown.
Roundel
The circular national insignia on Allied aircraft. The RAF's blue-white-red was the original.
Schräge Musik
"Slanted music" — upward-firing cannon fitted to Luftwaffe night-fighters to attack RAF heavies from below.
Split-S
Half-roll inverted into a half-loop downward. Rapid altitude loss with reversal of direction.
Tally-Ho RAF
Visual contact with the enemy made; engaging.
Vic
Three-aircraft V formation. Pretty in 1939, lethal by 1940 — abandoned after Dunkirk.
Wilco
"Will comply." Standard RAF/USAAF radio acknowledgement.
